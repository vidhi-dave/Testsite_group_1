Sodium Chlorate

Largely of historical interest.

Sources

Sodium chlorate was widely used as a nonselective weedkiller along railway tracks and roadsides, and on garden paths because it has a persistent effect. It is a strong oxidising agent which has been used to make illegal explosives so its use has been largely replaced by organic herbicides.

Toxicity

The oral toxicity is low (MLD cattle 1g/kg, sheep 1.5-2.5 g/kg and poultry 5g/kg bodyweight).

Pathophysiology

It converts haemoglobin to methaemoglobin and continues to do this even after death.

Clinical signs

The chlorates cause enteritis and severe abdominal pain. Haemoglobinuria and haematuria may be present. Animals will show signs very similar to nitrate/nitrite poisoning and hence similar treatments may be applied.

Treatment

They include intravenous dosage with methylene blue at 2 - 4 mg/kg.  Blood transfusions and gastric lavage can be used in monogastric animals as well as gut protectives.
