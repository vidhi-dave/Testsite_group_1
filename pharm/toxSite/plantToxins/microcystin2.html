Microcystin

Microcystin LR is produced by several common species of cyanobacteria or blue green algae. Poisoning was first reported in 1878 in sheep in Australia, but seems to be getting more common in NZ.

Sources

Microcystis aeruginosa is proabably the commonest source, but other genera of blue green algae such as Anabaena and Nodularia can also produce microcystin. These algae form blooms on ponds and lakes when the weather conditions are right; mammals are then poisoned by drinking the water (or inhaling aerosols). Stagnant water high in nutrients (N, P and Fe), combined with high water temperatures, encourage blue green algae growth. This means that most problems occur in summer in NZ.

Overseas data indicates that there are many different strains of Microcystis which grow under different conditions and produce varying amounts of microcystin. There is no published data for NZ yet. Most blue green algae also produce other toxins as well as microcystin.

Blooms form on the surface of the water as greenish scum which tends to get blown onto the downwind side of the pond. As the algae die, the scum turns brown. Microcystin is released from the algal cells as they die. It is stable in pond water for less than one week as it is broken down by bacteria and adsorbed onto organic material.

Structure

Microcystins are water soluble cyclic heptapeptides. There are more than 60 microcystins: they are normally differentiated by specifying the amino acids at the 2 and 4 position. Microcystin LR is the commonest and one of the most potent. Nodularin, from Nodularia spumigena, is similar, but has only five amino acids. Microcystin is chemically very stable but is broken down by bacteria.

Toxicity

LD50 (ip) in rats 72µg/kg; mice 50µg/kg (range 25 - 125µg/kg). Cattle have survived 13µg/kg/day in their water. It is toxic enough to aquatic insects to have been investigated as an insecticide - LD50s 0.05-5mg/kg. Microcystin also kills fish and many plants, including other algae.

WHO limit for drinking water (for people) is 1µg/L. This is recommended in NZ. Where water has been tested after outbreaks in NZ, there are usually mg/L present. In one case in the USA, 148mg/L caused sudden death in 24/175 heifers.

Pathophysiology

Microcystin is absorbed by the gut and preferentially taken up into liver cells by an active transport system. Its main targets are the serine/threonine protein phosphatases 1 and 2A, although other enzyme systems may also be affected. Excessive phosphorylation damages the cytoskeleton. Loss of adhesion to neighbouring cells causes collapse of the liver sinusoids and the liver becomes engorged with blood.

High concentrations of microcystin promote liver cell death induced by reactive oxygen species signalling without the involvement of typical apoptotic proteins, intermediate doses activate classic apoptotic pathways. Low concentrations however, increase liver cell survival and proliferation, and can cause primary liver cancer, probably by oxidative DNA damage.

Some microcystin also gets into the brain, where it can affect memory.

Clinical signs

Often sudden death is the main sign. Other signs in cattle include weakness, recumbency, anorexia and hypersensitivity to noise; which may rapidly deteriorate to coma and death. Rumen atony and diarrhoea have been reported in cattle. Photosensitisation frequently occurs in animals that survive for several days.

Post mortem signs

A markedly enlarged and often haemorrhagic liver, fluid accumulation in the body cavities, and widespread petechiae and ecchymoses. In peracute cases the liver is swollen, friable and congested. In acute cases the periacinar hepatocytes are necrotic with haemorrhage, while the remainder of the acinus shows hydropic degeneration or fatty change. Oedema and haemorrhage of the gall bladder wall may also be prominent. Nephrosis, splenomegaly, congestion of the viscera, lung oedema and fatty change in the myocardium are other lesions which may be encountered. Microscopic examination reveals centrilobular to near massive necrosis. In chronic cases, hepatic cirrhosis, icterus, photosensitivity, emaciation and ascites may be present.

Diagnosis

Clinical signs and the presence of an algal bloom are suggestive, post mortem signs will confirm the diagnosis. Acute cases will have raised liver enzymes. A sample of pond water with scum (500mL) should be taken as soon as possible. Microcystin can be detected in water and serum using ELISA or HPLC. Bioassays with bacteria, various plants and insects have also been used. PCR of the microcystin producing gene has been used to screen water supplies.

Differential diagnosis

Other toxicities causing liver failure or neurological signs.

Treatment

Treatment is unlikely to be sucessful in acute cases. Cholestyramine has been used to prevent further absorption in monogastric animals. The antibiotic rifampicin can block uptake of microcystin into liver cells and has been shown to protect mice when given 15 mins after exposure to a lethal dose of microcystin. Rifampicin 25mg/kg ip prevented death in 75% of mice but lower doses were less effective. Monoclonal antibodies have been used in people.

Animals should be moved away from the source of microcystin and provided with clean water and shade.

Prevention

Inspect water supplies daily in summer. Keep animals away from ponds with algal blooms. Blue green algae can be killed with copper sulphate in the water, but this releases microcystin from the cells, so animals (including dogs and children) must be kept away from the water for at least 2 days, and preferably a week, after treatment. Most purification systems for (human) drinking water rely on biodegradation, for instance, slow filtration over sand beds.

Vitamin E protects rats from the chronic effects of microcystin.

Prognosis

Prognosis is poor, usually fatal or severe illness. A long recuperation period can be expected for animals that survive.
