Other names

Blue green algae (from the colour of the first species identified)

Description

An ancient group of prokaryotic organisms that are found all over the world in environments as diverse as Antarctic soils and volcanic hot springs. There are approximately 2000 species worldwide. They are microscopically small, single celled organisms with some characteristics of algae such as the ability to perform photosynthesis, and some of bacteria such as the absence of a nucleus. Some aquatic cyanobacteria occur suspended in water (planktonic), while others attach themselves to substrates or other surfaces (benthic).
Cyanobacteria can exist as solitary, free living cells or as colonies of cells e.g.: Microcystis aeruginosa Cyanobacteria in water are able to move and many also process the ability to regulate their buoyancy via intracellular gas vacuoles. This allows them to move up and down in a water column to optimise their nutritional and photosynthetic requirements. Some species can also fix elementary nitrogen dissolved in water via cells known as hetercysts. This allows them to survive in water with low levels of nitrogen. When environmental conditions are favourable the cells will multiply and form algal blooms. Cyanobacteria occur naturally, but pollution with nutrients from agriculture and urbanization has lead to eutrophication of many water bodies, which in turn has lead to the proliferation of cyanobacteria. Many cyanobacteria species are integral parts of ecosystems and as such cause no problems and are not harmful to humans/animals. In fact some species actually have beneficial uses to humans e.g. Spirulina – a nutritional supplement taken by some people, which is high in proteins and various mineral and vitamins.

Distribution

Toxin

A small group of cyanobacteria (approx. 46 species) produce cyanotoxins the major source of biotoxins found in surface waters.
Cyanotoxins are a diverse group of natural toxins, both from a chemical and toxicological point of view. In spite of their aquatic origin cyanotoxins appear to be more hazardous to terrestrial mammals than aquatic biota. The ecological role of these toxins is not clear.
Mechanisms of cyanobacterial toxicity currently described and understood are very diverse and range from hepatotoxic, neurotoxic and dermatoxic effect to general inhibition of protein synthesis. Cyanotoxins fall into three broad groups of chemical structure, shown in Table 1.
1. Heptatotoxic cyclic peptides are the most frequently found and are primarily toxic to liver. Microcystins are the most commonly found cyanotoxins in waterways. Microcystins are a large group – there are currently about 60 different structural variants of microcystins. The best characterised and one of the most toxic variants is microcystin LR. Nodularin is produced only by the brackish water species of Nodularia spumigena.
2. Neurotoxic Alkaloids – very diverse in structure & toxicity and are unique to cyanobacteria. There is also one hepatoxic alkaloid – Cylindrospermopsins, which affects the liver.
3. Saxitoxins – also produced by dingoflagellates in the marine environment where they are known as the paralytic shellfish poisons.
4. LPS are found in many species of cyanobacteria and considered less toxic than the hepatotoxins. There is currently a lack of information on these toxins.

Some species are capable of producing more than one type of toxin e.g.: Anabaena sp.– this creates problems when trying to detect/monitor for cyanotoxins in waterways. Within potentially toxic species there are toxic and non toxic strains, thus just because cyanobacteria are found in a water body it does not necessarily mean that cyanotoxins will also be found. What triggers a strain to produce toxins is not fully understood but is believed to linked to the genetics of individual species.

Species affected

All species including people.

Clinical signs acute

Clinical signs depend on the quantity and type of toxin ingested. The range of clinical signs can be wide especially if more than one type of cyanotoxin is involved. There are no specific lesions in animals that die of cyanobacterial neurotoxicoses.
Anatoxin a mimics the effect of acetylcholine. The onset of clinical signs is abrupt (within 60 minutes). Signs include lethargy, muscle tremors, hyperpnoea, cyanosis, paralysis, hyperaesthesis, convulsions and death within 30 minutes after signs appear as a result of respiratory arrest.
Anatoxin–a (S) is an anticholinesterase, and has not been recorded in New Zealand. Exposure causes an increase in salivation, lacrimation, ataxia, diarrhoea, dyspnoea and death within an hour. Signs similar to anatoxin a may also occur.
Saxitoxins block nerve cell sodium channels. They cause paralysis, respiratory depression and respiratory failure. In people incoordination, confused speech, nausea, vomiting, eye irritation, and respiratory distress may occur. In severe cases respiratory paralysis requires artificial respiration.
Hepatotoxins (eg microcystins, nodularin) cause intrahepatic haemorrhage and liver damage. Sometimes convulsions, paralysis or muscle tremors precede death. Depending on the amount of toxin ingested signs can be acute or chronic. The liver may appear normal, but is usually enlarged and dark red from congestion and intrahepatic haemorrhage. Histologically, a widespread centrilobular to midzonal necrosis is observed.
Cylindrospermopsin blocks protein synthesis with a major effect on liver cells. Effects include extended fibrosis and bile duct proliferation with isolated groups of hepatocytes remaining. Extensive subserosal haemorrhages of heart, and extended epicardial haemorrhages of the small intestine and omentum.
