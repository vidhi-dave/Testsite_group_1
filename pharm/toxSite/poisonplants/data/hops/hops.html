Hops

Humulus lupulus

Other names

Description

Sprawling vine with shoots up to 10m covered in stiff hairs. Leaves 3-5 lobed. Usually dioecious, male flowers insignificant, female flowers (the part used in brewing) cone shaped, about 30mm long and with a distinctive smell. Flowers Jan - Mar. Plant dies down in winter and regrows from roots.

Similar plants

Related to cannabis, but the climbing habit is distinctive.

Distribution

Locally common in waste ground, grown as a crop (up stings tied to poles).

Toxin

Unknown. Hops contain essential oils (responsible for the smell) and various bitter compounds. The amounts of these vary with the variety. Fresh, dried, pelleted and used hops are all thought to be toxic.

Species affected

dogs

Clinical signs acute

A malignant hyperthermia-like syndrome: panting, agitation, vomiting, injected mucous membranes, tachycardia, high temperature and death

Clinical signs chronic

Post mortem signs

No specific signs.

Diagnosis

History, clinical findings.

Differential diagnosis

Treatment

Symptomatic - decontaminate and cool down. iv fluids and dantrolene may be useful.

Prognosis

Can be lethal. If the dog is going to die, it will be within 3 to 12 hours after the onset of clinical signs. When treatment is successful, clinical signs can take 24 to 72 hours to resolve.

Prevention

Prevent access to hops and dispose of used hops safely.
