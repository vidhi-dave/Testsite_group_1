Case 3: pony with Cushing's

Your neighbour's pony is a 26-year-old Welsh Mountain Pony that over the last 2 years has failed to shed its winter coat, has lost weight and has recurrent episodes of laminitis. You are suspicious the pony may have Pituitary Pars Intermedia dysfunction (PPID). There are several screening tests available that use drugs to help confirm this diagnosis. Please outline these tests and their potential risks and benefits. Many horses with PPID have been successfully managed with drugs. Please outline the different drugs available for treatment of this condition, their mechanisms of action, and also the potential cost to the client for each treatment.

My Answer

Tests

resting cortisol levels - useless

dexamethasone suppression test - specific but not very sensitive, depending on how it is done. May make laminitis worse.

ACTH stimulation test - useless in horses

TRH test - good if ACTH measured, but this is technically difficult. Less good but can be combined with DST if cortisol is measured.

high insulin - some use, but not specific.

Treatment

Sort out feet and teeth, clip coat, worm.

Surgical hypophysectomy - tricky

Cyproheptadine - unavailable and not very effective. It has multiple mechanisms of action.

Bromocriptine - works but low bioavailability. Cost 60c - $12/day depending on dose required. Dopamine agonist.

Pergolide - works well, most commonly used. Low dose used these days, cost about $1.70/day. Dopamine agonist. Best choice!

Mitotane - useless in horses and potentially dangerous to owner. Also unavailable in NZ and very expensive if it can be obtained from the UK. Adrenal cortex toxin.

Trilostane - not much known, but effective in dogs and people. Not available in NZ, can be obtained from the UK at huge cost. Prevents synthesis of corticosteroids.
