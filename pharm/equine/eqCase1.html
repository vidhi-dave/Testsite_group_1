Equine Clinical Pharmacology

Case Scenarios

Case 1: stallion with colic

You are called to examine a 5-year-old Stallion showing signs of colic. On initial examination the horse has a heart rate of 70 bpm, rectal temperature of 36.9°C, respiratory rate of 28, injected and dry mucous membranes with a CRT of 2.5 seconds, cold extremities and reduced gut sounds over all four abdominal quadrants. You are concerned that the horse may have an obstruction and would like to confirm this by passing a nasogastric tube and performing an examination per rectum. However, despite the severity of clinical signs, the stallion is difficult to handle and requires sedation. Outline your sedation options in this case and their potential complications.

My Answer

Object

Standing sedation and analgesia for 15 - 20 mins to allow examination. If the stallion is misbehaving because of pain, a drug which provides analgesia alone may be enough. No cardiovascular depression or gut effects. The effects should wear off before you leave the horse so you can reassess its pain.

options

α2 agonists

These provide sedation and analgesia but also constriction followed by relaxation of smooth muscle. The main difference is in duration of action: at normal doses; xylazine - 20mins, detomidine - 45mins, romifidine - 45 − 60mins.

Acepromazine

Minor sedation at the best of times, no analgesia. Unlikely to work.

Opioids

These provide analgesia rather than sedation, but may make the stallion stand still if it is excited because of pain. The only one short enough acting for this use is pethidine, which also acts as a spasmolytic. Butorphanol is long acting and may cause excitement - it should be given with an alpha 2 agonist, which spoils its point.

NSAIDs

Very effective analgesics, but too long acting for an initial examination. Best reserved for after a diagnosis has been made, in case the analgesia masks signs of the condition worsening.

My Choice

Xylazine.
