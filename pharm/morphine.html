Morphine

Morphine is an opioid, which are the main drugs used for severe pain in most species except ruminants (use α2agonists instead).

Effects of overdose in cats and horses can look like pain.It is a controlled drug class B1 under the Misuse of Drugs Act.

Indications

Severe pain. There is good evidence that it works better if given before pain starts, so it is often used as an anaesthetic premedication. It is also antitussive.

Contra-indications

There are no absolute contraindications, but: severe head injury? - opioids raise intracranial pressure upper respiratory tract injury? - blocking cough reflex may cause inhalation of blood clots, teeth, etc. unconsciousness - unable to feel pain??? Chest injury is sometimes cited as a contraindication for opioids on the theoretical grounds that they inhibit respiration, however, rib injuries are extremely painful and opioids often result in better respiration in animals with chest injuries.

Side effects

Excitement at high doses in cats and horses; respiratory depression in combination with anaesthetics; vomiting (segmental pruritus after epidural administration).

Care

Raises intracranial pressure; does not work well in ruminants; avoid iv in dogs (histamine release) (very slow infusion of dilute solutions acceptable)

Pharmacokinetics

Cats may metabolise morphine slowly: a mixture of analgesic and excitatory metabolites are probably produced in all species: duration of analgesia depends on pain rather than pharmacokinetics.

Dose

all spp: 0.1 - 1mg/kg sc / im (use low doses in horses); 0.1mg/kg (diluted in a suitable volume saline) epidurally
