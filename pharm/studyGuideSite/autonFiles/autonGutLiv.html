Liver disease

Acute liver disease can be caused by a wide variety of viruses, bacteria and parasites, depending on species. Chronic liver disease can be caused by poisons (including drugs), metabolic disease or parasites.

Treatment involves removing the cause and managing the hepatic insufficiency. Remember that drug metabolism may be grossly abnormal. Antibiotics used for bacterial infection include ampicillin, co-amoxyclav and cephalosporins. Corticosteroids are sometimes used but great care is required. Ursodeoxycholic acid is sometimes used in chronic liver disease in small animals. It increases excretion of bile acids. Bedlington terriers and Westies are susceptible to copper toxicosis and are treated with penicillamine to chelate copper.

Hepatic encephalopathy is usually caused by a shunt in the liver and is treated surgically, but sometimes medical treatment to reduce ammonia uptake from bacterial protein metabolism is required. The laxative lactulose is often used. Occasionaly antibiotics which are not absorbed from the gut such as neomycin are effective.

Exocrine pancreatic disease

This is common in dogs, particularly German Shepherds. It is usually treated by controlling the diet, and with pancreatin supplements, usually in the form of granules mixed with the food. Capsules are also available but are less effective.

Pancreatin is a mixture of proteases, lipases and amylases. Different preparations have slightly different proportions of each, and are not generally interchangeable. The dose must be tailored to the response of the individual dog.
