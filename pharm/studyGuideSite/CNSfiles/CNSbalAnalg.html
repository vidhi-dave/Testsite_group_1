Balanced analgesia

Balanced analgesia means using several drugs at low doses so that the analgesic effects are additive or synergistic but the side effects are reduced.

Combinations usually include an opioid or α2 agonist (or both) and an NSAID plus one or more of the following:

NMDA antagonists

Ketamine is the only drug easily available, tiletamine mixed with zolazepam (a benzodiazepine sedative) could be used in acute situations. Their analgesic effects only become obvious at anaesthetic doses but ketamine potentiates other analgesic drugs at very low doses. Ketamine is an NMDA antagonist. It stops / prevents wind up in spinal cord and is also amnesic in man (blocks memory of pain). High doses in combination with a2 agonists will cause anaesthesia.

Tricyclic antidepressants block reuptake of noradrenaline and 5HT which then act on α2 and 5HT3 receptors in the dorsal horn of the spinal cord. Amitriptyline works best in man at doses well below the antidepressant dose, probably because it is a potent NMDA blocker at clinical doses, and this probably accounts for most of its analgesic action.

Various other drugs, eg, the antiviral amantadine, also block NMDA receptors and have been used for balanced analgesia. Probably better to stick to amitriptyline and save amantadine for treating 'flu outbreaks.

Inhalation anaesthetics

All cause unconsciousness but some cause profound analgesia at low doses. Nitrous oxide is the most widely used (50% mixture with oxygen is probably the single safest analgesic). Methoxyflurane (not available in NZ) and trichloroethylene are good analgesics at low doses. Their mechanism(s?) are unknown but may involve endogenous opioids - naloxone will reverse nitrous oxide analgesia. Low dose isoflurane or sevoflurane may also work.

Sodium channel blockers

These are used for causalgia in people. Lignocaine given intravenously has been used this way in hores, but is metabolised too quickly after systemic administration to be much use unless given by infusion. Longer acting sodium channel blockers(see antiarrhythmic drugs) are used in people after testing the effects of iv lignocaine. Newer drugs such as gabapentin also probably work by the same mechanism and are used in people for neurogenic pain.

Topical analgesics
Capsaicin (the hot substance in chillies) depletes C fibres of substance P - causes burning pain first but then blocks pain for days - months.
EMLA cream - eutectic mixture of local anaesthetics (lignocaine and prilocaine) - will penetrate skin to cause analgesia. Used in needle shy children, but also useful for repeated blood sampling etc. Useful in laboratory animals. (see also drugs used in the eye)

Dopamine antagonists

Butyrophenones such as droperidol (usually used as sedatives) produce deep sedation in combination with opioids. Enhanced analgesic effects have been reported but are dubious.

Combinations of drugs should be chosen for the circumstances of the individual animal. Pain changes with time: different drugs and combinations may work more effectively at different times. Remember local analgesia.

The aim of giving combinations of analgesics is to achieve synergy. This is practically impossible to show clinically and very difficult experimentally, partly because there is no widely accepted statistical technique. So far there is only good experimental evidence for synergy between opioids and α2 agonists (but only by the spinal route) and opioids and NSAIDs. This is a hot research area so things will change in the near future!
