Determining Structure

Spectroscopy provides the organic chemist with a variety of ways to find out about different aspects of a molecule's structure. We have looked at how one spectroscopic technique, 1H NMR spectroscopy, can provide information about the carbon-hydrogen framework of a molecule. The technique can tell us the number of sets of chemically rquivalent hydrogens in the molecule, what other atoms or groups of atoms are close to those carbon-hydrogen bonds (chemical shift) and the relative numbers of hydrogens of each type in the molecule (integration).

However, 1H NMR spectroscopy cannot tell us much about groups which do not contain hydrogen such as the carbonyl (CO) group or the cyano (CN) group. Other spectroscopic techniques such as infrared spectroscopy (What functional groups are present?) and mass spectrometry (What size and formula?) provide complementary information about molecular structure.

The infrared region thus spans 10 000 cm-1 to 100 cm-1. For organic molecules, we typically view the region from 400 cm-1 to 4000 cm-1.

Instead of plotting absorbance vs wavenumber for an infrared spectrum, it is conventional to plot the inverse, how much light is transmitted by the sample (% transmittance) through the sample versus the wavenumber (cm-1)
The frequency of the electromagnetic radiation absorbed in the IR region corresponds to a bond vibration, perhaps a stretching or bending of a bond.

Click here to see some of the different vibrations in methanol.

When a molecule is irradiated with infrared light, it can absorb energy if the frequency of the light and the frequency of one of it's vibrating bonds is the same. The absorption of energy will increase the amplitude and the frequency of the bond vibration.

Since almost all molecular vibrations will result in absorption of infrared light, the typical infrared spectrum is a complex multitude of absorption bands.

Fortunately, absorption bands due to characteristic bond vibrations can usually be identified among the many bands. In addition, the positions of functional group absorptions vary little from molecule to molecule so that infrared spectroscopy is particularly useful for the identification of functional groups.

The complex absorption band pattern of each organic molecule provides a 'fingerprint' for that molecule. Thus, an infrared spectrum is an invaluable aid to molecular identification. Infrared spectra are molecular fingerprints.

 

If the infrared spectra of two similar molecules (for example cyclopentanone in red and cyclohexanone in blue) are compared, it is clear that each spectrum is a unique 'fingerprint' of the molecule.

Some features of these spectra should be noted.

  • The lower wavenumber scale is not linear - from 600 to 2000 cm-1 is graduated every 200 cm-1 whereas from 2000 to 5000 cm-1 is graduated every 1000 cm-1.
  • The majority of absorption bands occur between 800 and 1500 cm-1 and this area is known as the fingerprint region.
  • In the 1500 to 3800 cm-1 area, most functional group absorption bands occur. Therefore, it is often only the absorption bands in this area that are of interest, simplifying the interpretation of the spectrum.

Mass spectrometry

“a mass spectrometrist is someone who figures out what something is by smashing it with a hammer and looking at the pieces”

JEOL (manufacturer) website

Mass spectrometry provides information on the size (mass) and formula of molecules as well as some structural information. In this lecture, we shall only consider how the molecular mass of a molecule can be determined.

Even the name suggests that mass spectrometry is unlike the other spectroscopic techniques; electromagnetic radiation is not involved nor is there an absorption of energy.

In the mass spectrometer, a substance is bombarded with a beam of high-energy electrons. This causes an electron to be ejected from the molecule creating a charged species called the molecular ion ( )

The ejected electron will come from a bond or from a nonbonded pair of electrons. For example, for ammonia (NH3):

Since the mass of an electron is insignificant compared to that of the rest of the ion, the mass of the ion is the same as the mass of the original molecule. The high energy electron beam also leads to fragmentation of molecules by breaking bonds. The nature of the fragmentation can give clues as to the structure of the molecule.

Molecular Mass from a Mass Spectrum

Although the detailed workings of a mass spectrometer are beyond the scope of this course, it is important to understand some aspects of the collection of a mass spectrum.

The large number of positive molecular ions generated in the mass spectrometer are focused between the poles of a magnet. Ions of different mass are deflected by different amounts in the magnetic field such that the ion detector can distinguish between different masses. Ions with higher mass-to-charge (m/z) ratio move down the chamber faster.

Thus the mass of the molecular ion can be measured. Since the molecular ion differs from the parent molecule by only one electron, the mass of parent molecule is obtained.

Isotopes

Different isotopes of atoms contain different numbers of neutrons and vary in their mass.  This can be detected by mass spectrometry.

The relative abundances of 12C and 1H are close to 100%. Thus for a hydrocarbon like cyclohexane we see predominantly one line for the molecular ion corresponding to the molecular weight. For cyclohexane, molecular formula C6H12, this will be at m/z 84 (6 x 12 + 12 x 1).

C6H12  +  e–  ®  [C6H12]·+  +  2e–

You will notice a very small line at m/z 85. This corresponds to the small number of molecules that contain one 13C atom.
