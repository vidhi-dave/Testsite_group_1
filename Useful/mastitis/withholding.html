Withholding Times - Residues - Recording



All products that are registered for use in dairy cattle have milk and meat withholding times on the label. A milk withholding time is the time after treatment that the milk must be discarded.

Hefty penalties are imposed if farmers are found to have unacceptable levels of anti-biotics or other "inhibitory substances" in milk supplied to the factory.


The following recommendations should prevent contamination of bulk milk:

  • Read the label before administering any drug to an animal.

  • Animals which are treated must be clearly identified and run separately from the main milking herd. They should also be milked separately after the milk line has been diverted from the vat, to avoid contamination of the bulk milk supply.

  • It is now illegal to use quarter milkers and test buckets to intercept contaminated milk from treated cows milked amongst the main herd. Quarter milkers intercept milk from the treated quarter only, there is clear evidence that antibiotic from a treated quarter can be absorbed systemically by the animal and re-secreted into the other quarters. Test buckets are not recommended because residues from treated cows can remain in the cluster and milk line which will go into the vat once the cluster is re-connected to the main milk line. Also test-buckets can over flow or be tipped over allowing milk to be sucked into the main milk line.

  • Registered withholding times are not applicable to "off-label" use of the product. e.g. If more treatments are given than stated on the label, or If more than one quarter is treated it is recommended to advise a longer withholding time. Unfortunately the time is up to the discretion of the farmer or veterinarian.

  • Wash the plant with hot water after each milking.


Despite the penalties and recommendations, mistakes are still common and milk contaminated with antibiotics finds its way into the vat.

Most contamination is the result of human error e.g:

  • treated animals not being separated,
  • treated animals escaping back into herd where they are not recognised,
  • milk-line to vat not diverted before treated cows are milked.
  • Use of test-buckets to intercept contaminated milk.


Cases have occurred where the source of contamination is less obvious:

  • Multiple quarter treatments increasing withholding times.
  • Multiple cow treatments resulting in enough antibiotic reaching the vat after the end of the withholding time to fail an inhibitory substance test.
  • Carry over of antibiotics in milk deposits in the plant if washing is not adequate.

Don't forget that mastitis preparations for lactational and dry cow treatment have meat withholding times. This is the period after treatment before which the animal can be slaughtered for human consumption.
This is also applicable to bobby calves.
If a bobby calf is born within the meat withholding time of a product (unless it has a stated bobby calf withholding time) then it must be withheld from slaughter until the witholding time for the dam has elapsed.
If a bobby calf drinks milk contaminated with antibiotics from a treated cow, then the calf must be witheld from slaughter and fed clean milk for seven days.

It is now a legal requirement and a requirement of supply to the dairy factory that all details of animal treatments be recorded by the farmer. The veterinarian has a significant role to play in transfer of the appropriate and accurate information and instructions to the farmer about treatments given / to be given and their withholding periods.
