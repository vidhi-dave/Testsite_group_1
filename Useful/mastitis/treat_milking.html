Which Milking Cow treatment to choose? - in practice



A wide variety of milking cow intra-mammary preparations are available in New Zealand. Fortunately for the new veterinarian, most practices only stock a selection of these. A variety of different drug companies produce mastitis preparations and many products have the same or similar active ingredients.

See the Table of Milking Cow Preparations

The products available in New Zealand can be grouped broadly into the following categories:

  • Penicillin
  • Penicillin/ Streptomycin combination
  • Semi-synthetic penicillins
  • Erythromycin
  • Cephalosporins
  • Tetracycline
  • Lincomycin
Some of these groups contain many brands but some are represented by only a single brand.
Some brands contain similar anti-microbial drugs in different concentrations which affects persistence in the udder, this is usually reflected in the length of the milk with-holding time.

The decision about which actual brands to stock is usually made by senior veterinarians in a practice based upon:
  • Knowledge of the drugs pharmacology.
  • Local knowledge and previous experience with mastitis preparations .
  • Culture and sensitivity results.
  • Preference of drug company to deal with.
  • Cost.
Different practices use different products as first choice mastitis preparations and reserve some drugs to treat refractory cases.

It is important that the new veterinarian in a practice familiarise themselves with which products are in stock and with the practices protocol for treatment of mastitis.

Often the farmer is supplied with their regular mastitis preparations upon request, and the veterinarian does not become involved in the treatment of mastitis unless the farm is experiencing a problem. Or animals are not responding to the regular preparation.

At this point a number of options exist to the veterinarian, including:
  • Visiting the farm to examine the herd or the problem animals depending upon the situation.
  • Obtaining milk samples of mastitis cases for culture and anti-microbial sensitivity testing.
  • Changing to a different brand of intra-mammary preparation , either longer persistence in the udder or different family of drug.
  • Using supportive treatment for individual cows such as parenteral anti-microbial drugs and or oxytocin, NSAID's.
Remember though that intramammary antibiotics are prescription animal remedies that must only be supplied to bona-fide clients of the practice following a consultation with a veterinarian. Most practices do readily supply intramammary antibiotics to clients based on discussions that have occurred at some time in the past and the veterinarians and farmers knowledge of the responses to treatment. Some practices formalise this arrangement with actual written prescriptions.
