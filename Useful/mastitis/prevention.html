Prevention

Prevention of mastitis requires an understanding of the epidemiology of mastitis.

The ideal means of dealing with mastitis is to prevent it from happening. However, even under the best prevention and control programs, mastitis will occur.

After mastitis has been detected and the causative agent identified it is important to focus on preventing transmission - often by removing the source of contamination.

Mastitis prevention requires a daily commitment from milk producers to maintain a high standard of cleanliness in the cows' environment and to prevent teat injuries.

When high somatic cell counts, or a high incidence of clinical cases indicate a herd problem, making appropriate changes in herd management and housing can significantly improve the production and profitability of a dairy herd.

An effective mastitis management program includes:

Establishment of goals for udder health

  • Set realistic targets
  • Review and update control procedures regularly
  • Change management to achieve stated goals

Prevention of new infections

  • Reduce environmental contamination and reduce the exposure of the teat ends to causal organisms
  • Ensure proper maintenance and function of milking equipment
  • Disinfect teats routinely with effective disinfectants
  • Use recommended milking procedures
  • Provide dry cow therapy
  • Implement milking order and segregation programs
  • Use preventive management of the replacement, dry and lactating animals
  • Carefully introduce new animals to the herd after testing or quarantine, or maintain a closed herd
  • Increase resistance factors in the udder

Eliminate existing infections

  • Lactating cow therapy
  • Dry cow therapy
  • Culling of chronically infected cows

Monitor udder health

  • Bulk milk somatic cell count program
  • Individual cow somatic cell count (SCC) program
  • Regular visual inspection and palpation of the udders
  • Rapid Mastitis Test (CMT) or other cow tests for detection of subclinical mastitis
  • Use laboratory culture for detection of organisms

Record keeping

Keep records of mastitis incidence, control and monitoring information.

Mastitis prevention plans

There are many national mastitis management programmes. They are all based on the 5-point plan introduced in the early 1970s in the UK. Before the 5-point plan, which was based on sound research, there was no consistent approach to preventing mastitis. The components of the 5-point plan are:

  • Disinfect every teat after every milking
  • Treat clinical mastitis promptly
  • Give dry cow therapy to all quarters that remain in the herd at drying off
  • Cull cows with repeated cases of mastitis
  • Test, repair and maintain the milking machine regularly

The 5-point plan is still relevant today; but more emphasis is placed on sub-clinical mastitis as identified by SCC. Improved control also means modern herds have a lower incidence of mastitis.

The seasonality of the pasture-based dairy system in New Zealand have lead to the development of a national mastitis control programme- the SAMM plan. This programme divides the lactation cycle into critical periods for mastitis control and describes activities to minimise mastitis in these periods.

The 6 risk periods in the SAMM plan are identified as:

  • Late lactation period
  • Drying-off period (see The Golden Rules of drying-off)
  • Dry-period
  • Calving period
  • Colostrum period
  • Lactation period

The SAMM Plan provides state of the art advice for mastitis control in New Zealand herds. The information has been provided in a booklet from:

Seasonal calving in New Zealand gives an opportunity for uninfected heifers coming into the herd to stay free of infection if not exposed to contamination from the older cows. Ideally, all cows in the herd should start the season uninfected to minimise the risk of infection spread, particularly by contagious organisms. Procedures, such as culling and DCT should help to achieve this.

Prevention of new infections

Control of the environment and reduction of exposure of the teat ends to causal organisms.

Environment

To reduce the exposure of teat ends to the mastitis causing organisms, keep the environment clean and dry. This simple concept of prevention can be very difficult to achieve. The demands on dairy farmers to manage labour to maintain a clean, dry environment for cattle competes with other needs of the dairy enterprise. Trends towards increased herd size, housing, greater milk production and genetic selection toward production and away from disease resistance are all modern dairy management realities. They work against steps to control environmental mastitis. Pastures can also pose exposure problems, especially after periods of excessive rainfall. Disinfectants and cleaning procedures cannot practically overcome massive environmental contamination.

Proper maintenance and function of milking equipment

The milking machine is integral to efficiency on a dairy farm and has direct contact with teats. It must perform properly and consistently every day, year after year. Milking systems should be installed following approved guidelines. Maintenance should be carried out regularly and machine function evaluated by scheduled periodic analysis of the system in a milking machine test. Each person involved in milking should thoroughly understand the components, function and operation of milking equipment. They should also be aware of the significance of regular equipment maintenance and of the importance of proper milking technique. Recommended vacuum levels and pulsation ratios and patterns must be maintained. Damaged rubber ware must be replaced immediately. Liners should be replaced at least twice per year or after each 2500 milkings. Service checks should occur every 6 months, or minimally annually, depending on the hours per day the system runs.

Regular teat disinfection with an effective disinfectant

The objective of teat disinfection is to kill organisms residing on teats and thereby reduce the rate of new intramammary infections. Proper teat disinfection requires that teats are dipped or sprayed with a clean disinfectant solution immediately after milking ensuring that teat ends and lateral surfaces are well covered. Post-milking teat disinfection is proven to decrease new intra-mammary infections with contagious and to a lesser degree, environmental organisms. Post-milking teat disinfection must be done correctly and consistently to achieve its maximum impact.

Using recommended milking procedures

Milking hygiene is extremely important because of the potential interaction between milking machine function and bacteria on the teat skin. When attaching teat-cups the amount of air drawn into the milking system must be minimised. Too much air causes random vacuum fluctuations that can generate milk aerosol impacts on the teat end causing machine-induced infections. Cluster alignment should be adjusted as necessary during milking to prevent air entering the system (causing squawking) and cups dropping or being kicked off teats. Alignment also ensures that the quarters milk out properly. Once milking is completed in a cow, the vacuum to the milking unit should be shut off and the teat-cups removed. Removing the unit under vacuum causes milk and air to impact onto the teat ends. Cleaning and sanitising of the cups after every milking is important in reducing the number of mastitis-causing organisms and in maintaining milk quality.

Personal hygiene of milkers

Milkers are sometimes casual workers who may be inadequately trained in milk quality management. Milkers’ hands should be thoroughly washed with soap and water and dried before milking begins. If hands become soiled during milking, they must be washed and dried again. Clean, dry, healthy hands minimize the spread of mastitis-causing organisms between cows. The wearing of milking gloves reduces transmission of mastitis causative agents. Milking uniforms must be as clean as possible.

Water supply

Water sources, tanks, pipes, heaters and hoses may be become contaminated with environmental organisms such as coliforms, Pseudomonas sp, etc. Worn hoses and pipes should be replaced.

Preventative management of replacement, dry and lactating animals

Minimise social and management changes for heifers and cows near calving to minimise stress. Cows do not like change.

Replacement animals

Heifers should calve "clean" of contagious intramammary infections due to a lack of exposure. However, some herds do have significant levels of infection in first calving heifers. The management of calves and yearlings influences mastitis occurrence in heifers. Treatment with antibiotics or teat sealants in all quarters of all heifers before parturition has been shown to decrease the prevalence of mastitis in heifers at calving.

Dry cows

Dry cow management strategies ideally should reduce the risk of infection from last milking until the next calving. The rate of new intramammary infections in untreated dry cows is expected to be between 8% and 12% of quarters. The objective of udder health management in the dry period is to minimise infected quarters at calving. Thus, infections present at the time of drying off should be eliminated and new intra-mammary infections during the dry period prevented. The focus of mastitis control schemes for the dry period is often on contagious pathogens but environmental bacteria need to be considered. Exposure to environmental pathogens appears to continue throughout the dry period making the prevention of new dry period infection with environmental organisms a considerable challenge. Some recent research data shows that daily or twice weekly application of post-milking teat disinfectants applied in the late dry period demonstrated considerable promise for controlling environmental infections.

Lactating cows

The Calving period. Maintenance of a clean environment is a key aspect of mastitis control during this period. Avoid excessive contamination of bedding material especially in wet weather. Cows close to calving should be separated from other cows. Clip or flame udder hair prior to calving. Use properly functioning milking equipment on fresh cows. Sucking by calves may spread infection from quarter to quarter or cow to cow and may also be a factor in spreading the disease to other calves if they are allowed to suck each other.

Carefully introduce new animals to the herd

Care should be taken when purchasing replacements. Many herds become newly infected by adding cows with infected udders. New arrivals should be sampled before joining the milking herd and should be milked separately until their infection status is determined. The RMT offers a quick and effective means of screening cows before admitting them to the milking herd.

Dry cow therapy

The dry period offers a valuable opportunity to eliminate intramammary infections while cows are not lactating.

Management practices

Milking infected cows after the milking herd is an effective management practice that reduces the exposure of the healthy udders to contagious organisms. If cows have access to feed following milking they remain standing which allows the teat end to dry and the teat canal to close completely. This helps avoid environmental contamination immediately after milking. Milking wet and/or dirty teats increases the risk of high bacterial counts in milk and also increases the probability of new IMI.

Establish milking order and segregation program

After detection of clinical mastitis case the cow should be drafted out and milked last. This is very effective in controlling the spread of contagious mastitis. Segregation is not a simple, single solution to contagious mastitis problems. Ideally first-lactation heifers, fresh cows and cows with low SCC should be milked first followed by cows with a high SCC, chronic clinical mastitis and finally current clinical cases. This will result in reduced chance of spreading mastitis-causing organisms from cow-to-cow.

Increasing of resistance factors of the udder

Non-specific resistance factors

The teat sphincter is a primary physical barrier to infection. Once this defence is breached, secondary and less effective immune mechanisms are mobilised. Key to the secondary line of defence are neutrophils. Genetic variations in resistance to mastitis have been proven. One inherited characteristic which may affect susceptibility to mastitis is teat shape and cows with cylindrical teats become infected more commonly than cows with funnel-shaped teats. The shape of the teat end may also be important. Cows with pointed teat ends appear to have smaller diameter teat canal, and those with inverted, funnel-shaped ends, or with a recessed plate-like end appear to be more susceptible to infection. Other characters, which should be selected against are deep udders, excessively low hindquarters, widely placed teats, rear teats too far back and short wide teats.

Management of teat lesions

The ability of certain bacteria to colonise teat skin and thus be in a position to invade the mammary gland is documented. Teat lesions are common sites for such colonisation but few farmers appreciate their importance in mastitis control. Chapping of the teats is caused by wind, cold, wetness, sunburn and chemical irritation from teat sanitisers. Photosensitive dermatitis on the lateral aspects of light coloured teats, cowpox, pseudocowpox, bovine herpes mammillitis, black spot and papillomatosis are further common lesions. Treatment with a suitable antiseptic cream after each milking is recommended. lodophor teat disinfectant solutions containing 15% glycerine as an effective emollient assist the cure of sores and chapped teats.

Specific resistance factors

Vaccination.- Currently an effective vaccination programme is only available against E. coli mastitis in housed cows.

Concurrent disease

The increased incidence of diseases such as hypocalcaemia, ketosis and displaced abomasums are associated with an increased incidence of clinical mastitis. Thus, poorly managed nutrition of dry cows, pre-partum cows and cows in the first 60 days of lactation can adversely affect udder health.

Nutrition

Vitamin E, selenium and zinc supplements in diets appear to have a preventive effect against acute intramammary infections.

Elimination of the existing infections

Complete elimination of mastitis from a herd is practically impossible in the medium term. Mastitis due to Streptococcus agalactiae can however be eradicated from individual herds.

Lactating cow therapy

The treatment of all clinical cases as they occur is mandatory. The treatment of subclinical cases should be delayed until the dry period. If a cow is heavy producer, is in early lactation and subclinically infected with Strep. agalactiae then treatment may be advisable. If the infection rate with Streptococci or Mycoplasma in the herd is high, treatment of subclinical cases to reduce the source of infection may be advantageous. If infection in the herd is predominantly with Staph. aureus, treatment of subclinical cases is generally not effective. Treatment options depend on the signs and causal agent. Usually local use of intramammary antibiotics is recommended. Supportive care is necessary in peracute and acute cases.

Subclinical mastitis does not present an urgent threat to gland function or the life of the cow. Therapy is administered on the premise that treatment costs will be outweighed by compensatory production gains after elimination of infection but studies on the cost effectiveness of this approach do not support this. In the case of contagious organisms, elimination of infection may reduce the risk of new infections in previously non-infected cows. The degree of therapeutic success in treating subclinical mastitis relies predominantly on the type of causal organism and not often on the drug regimen selected. Parenteral in addition to intra-mammary therapy of chronic, subclinical intramammary infections may have the best probability of success. Any therapy should be administered for long enough periods to ensure drug levels above the MIC in order to allow effective killing of the causal pathogens. However, the cure rates achieved may not be much better than those attained from spontaneous cure. Antimicrobial therapy is not recommended for subclinical intramammary infections in lactating cows except for treating Strep. agalactiae.

Culling of chronically infected cows

The maintenance of a high culling rate of senior cows with lumpy udders, or a history of repeated mastitis is probably one of the most important steps in mastitis control programs in herds where the prevalence of mastitis is high. Culling is an important means of decreasing the reservoir of infection particularly of contagious organisms. Individual treatment records and individual somatic cell count data is an important prerequisite for sound culling decisions. Quarters, which do not respond to treatment, should be permanently dried up or the affected cows culled. Culling is a very effective method of decreasing the prevalence of chronically infected cows. It must be done judiciously with respect to production, breeding performance, genetic potential and other health problems. Excessive culling to achieve quality milk premiums might have a negative financial impact on herds.

Monitoring of udder health

A policy to detect or select subclinical infections is needed for selective dry cow therapy to be practiced. Another monitoring need in mastitis control is to identify the organisms infecting quarters.

Somatic cell count program

This comprises checks available at the herd, cow and quarter levels.

Herd level

This is done by means of a bulk tank somatic cell count. It indicates herds with mastitis problems.

Cow level

Cow-side tests or in-line electronic equipment can examine the milk of individual cows relatively economically. Monitoring SCC scores, particularly at dry off and calving can monitor mastitis control effectiveness. Individual cow somatic cell counts are useful guides for treatment and culling decisions.

Quarter level

Collection and examination of milk from all quarters of each cow, is usually arranged after individual cows have been identified as probably infected by a cow level check. Such checking provides more accurate data for assessing the mastitis status of individual cows.

Regular viewing and palpation of the udder

Diagnosis of mastitis in a non-lactating heifer, or cow, is not at an ideal time for treatment but adds to control. The simplest method is to palpate each quarter to detect differences between them. Infected quarters are usually swollen and firmer than non-infected quarters. Apparently infected quarters can be sampled for bacteriological testing.

Evaluation of teat condition on a regular basis to determine effects of equipment, system settings, teat sanitizers and the weather is important in mastitis control. Cows kept in warm, dry conditions have few if any significant changes to teat skin condition over the year.

Rapid Mastitis Test (CMT) or other cow side tests for detection of mastitis.

Bacteriological culture to identify organisms

Culture of milk samples from representative cows in the herd, including all cows when they show clinical mastitis, cows with high SCC and cows with chronic mastitis, allows bacteria causing mastitis to be identified and monitored. This knowledge can assist in determining the causes of mastitis and in developing further control strategies. Antimicrobial sensitivity testing should be included to determine if antibiotic resistance in bacteria is occurring for lactating ordry cow treatments.

Record keeping

The cow number, date, quarter infected and treatment must be recorded for cases of mastitis. Summaries of these records monthly and annually allow herd mastitis control to be monitored. These records can guide management and treatment decisions, such as cows to cull and treat with dry cow products.
