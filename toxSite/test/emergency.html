  • Remove the source of the poison

  • Supportive treatment

1 Remove the source of poison and deal with any life threatening conditions (convulsions, respiratory depression, shock).

2 Decontaminate the animal to limit the further absorption of the poison and hasten its elimination from the patient's body.

3 Supportive therapy to reduce the effects of the poison, particularly for convulsions and shock. If the poison has been identified apply specific antidotes if they are available.

1 Remove the poison

Removal of the animal from the source of poison, if it is known, is most important. In the case of grazing animals and animals which have died in a certain paddock, the rest of the flock or herd should be quickly and quietly moved to a new location. Such a move may be only temporary for example, in nitrate/nitrite poisoning the toxic level of some pasture species is only high for a brief period. Alternatively if the source of poison is not known then time is gained while the environment is thoroughly searched for the toxic plants or material. The provision of clean fresh water in such instances is also important as many toxicological conditions cause severe dehydration of the affected animals.

In the case of pets or small animals the client should be instructed to keep the animal quiet and warm. In some cases instructions such as muzzling a large dog may be necessary to avoid the owner or other onlookers from being hurt. (Many poisoned animals have violent nervous signs which are quite out of character with the animals' usual demeanour). If the source of the poison is obvious, for instance a package of slug pellets, the owner should be instructed to retain this and bring it to the clinic with the animal.

3 Supportive treatment

If the animal is unconscious, check its airway, breathing and circulation. If necessary, intubate its trachea, ventilate with oxygen and consider iv fluids and positive inotropes.

If the animal is convulsing, give midazolam 0.2mg/kg or diazepam 0.5mg/kg iv slowly and repeat if necessary.

If the animal is in shock, insert an iv catheter, by cut down if necessary, and give iv fluids (preferably colloids, but any type will do in an emergency) at up to 90mL/kg/h.

Once the animal has been stabilised, a diagnosis should be made and the definitive treatment given.
