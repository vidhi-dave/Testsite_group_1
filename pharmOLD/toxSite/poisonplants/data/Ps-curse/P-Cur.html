Paterson's curse

Echium plantagineum

Other names

Salivation Jane (Australia)

Description

A small plant which grows up to 30cm high and is found in poorer soils. It bears masses of small blue flowers which dry to copious numbers of seed heads.

Similar plants

Other borages, particularly viper's bugloss.

Distribution

Widely distributed, but frequently found in drier areas, waste places and by the roadside. It is common in the South Island.

Toxin

pyrrolizidine alkaloids

Species affected

Sheep and horses in Australia - not reported in NZ. The disease is responsible for serious and economic horse losses, particularly in South Australia where 220 cases were reported by veterinarians in 1983.

Clinical signs - chronic

Toxicosis as a result of eating feed containing pyrrolizidine alkaloids is common in sheep in Australia. In New Zealand, seneciosis in sheep is less common than in cattle and invariably causes chronic hepatocyte damage with enhanced uptake of copper leading to chronic copper poisoning (see chronic copper poisoning of sheep). Sheep enzymatically detoxify the ragwort alkaloids in the rumen to some extent, (no such activity occurs in cattle) and while growing sheep are susceptible to chronic ragwort poisoning, they are less so than cattle.

Post mortem signs

Ascites with oedema of the mesentery and associated intestinal and gall bladder walls and a small fibrotic liver. Jaundice may or may not be present and there are usually diffuse subendocardial haemorrhages. Histopathological changes in the liver usually include atrophy of the liver parenchyma, with zonal or diffuse megalocytosis of the hepatocytes; biliary duct hyperplasia with associated postal tract fibrosis, and perivascular fibrosis, affecting central veins.
