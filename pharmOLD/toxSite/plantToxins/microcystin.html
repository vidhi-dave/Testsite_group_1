Microcystin LR is produced by several common species of cyanobacteria or blue green algae. Poisoning was first reported in 1878 in sheep in Australia, but seems to be getting more common in NZ.

Sources

top  

Microcystis aeruginosa is proabably the commonest source, but other genera of blue green algae such as Anabaena and Nodularia can also produce microcystin. These algae form blooms on ponds and lakes when the weather conditions are right; mammals are then poisoned by drinking the water (or inhaling aerosols). Stagnant water high in nutrients, combined with high water temperatures, encourage blue green algae growth. Most blue green algae also produce other toxins as well as microcystin.

Blooms form on the surface of the water as greenish scum which tends to get blown onto the downwind side of the pond. As the algae die, the scum turns brown.

Microcystin is released from the algal cells as they die. It is stable in pond water for less than one week as it is broken down by bacteria and adsorbed onto organic material.

Structure and formulation

top  

Microcystins are water soluble cyclic heptapeptides. There are more than 50 microcystins: they are normally differentiated by specifying the amino acids at the 2 and 4 position. Microcystin LR is the commonest and one of the most potent. Nodularin, from Nodularia spumigena, is similar, but has only five amino acids.
Toxicity

top  

WHO limit for drinking water (for people) is 1µg/L. LD50 (ip) in rats 72µg/kg. It is toxic enough to aquatic insects to have been investigated as an insecticide.

Prevention

top  

Keep animals away from ponds with algal blooms. Blue green algae can be killed with copper sulphate in the water, but this releases microcystin from the cells, so animals (and children) must be kept away from the water for at least 2 days, and preferably a week, after treatment.
