NSAIDs side effects

Gut ulcers
Kidney damage
Other effects

Toxic doses and effective doses usually overlap. Major side effects include gut ulceration which is common but rarely serious - at any rate in animals. Dogs seem to be more prone to the gut side effects of NSAIDs than most species, possibly because many of the NSAIDs undergo entero hepatic cycling in the dog, but death from gut ulceration following NSAIDs is almost unknown (1200 people a year die from this in the UK - no figures for NZ).

The other major side effect is kidney failure. Inhibition of prostaglandin synthesis in the kidney of a healthy, well hydrated animal is probably of little consequence, and some predisposing factors has to be present.

gastric ulceration

diagram

This limits the use of most drugs to about 5 days at normal doses. Very common but not usually serious. Normal production of mucus in the stomach depends on PGE2 (produced by COX1), if mucus production is stopped then the stomach acid will cause ulceration. This can be prevented by giving PG analogues or PGE1 with the NSAID (gut pharmacology notes).

kidney damage

diagram

This usually only happens in combination with other factors:

hypotension / hypovolaemia (shock, poor anaesthesia, etc.)
chronic kidney failure
old age
urinary tract obstruction
sodium retention eg congestive heart disease

Kidney failure is rare but often fatal. When the mean pressure in the renal artery falls below about 65mmHg there is not enough pressure across the glomerulus for filtration to take place. The body responds by producing angiotensin II which constricts the efferent arteriole to keep the pressure in the glomerulus up, but in the long term, this would reduce blood flow through the kidney and cause ischaemic damage. To stop this, PGE2 causes emergency vasodilatation in the kidney (until blocked by NSAIDs). NSAID induced renal failure can usually be prevented by keeping the blood pressure at normal levels (usually with iv fluids).

The papilliary necrosis associated with NSAIDs is probably caused by interference with COX2, although COX1 may also be involved. COX2 knockout mice die of kidney failure, but of a different sort.

other side effects

increased bleeding time (mainly aspirin)

Although some drugs have specific hepatotoxic effects (eg, naproxen in dogs), idiosyncratic reactions sometimes take this form in dogs. This is probably an immune mediated problem, but is too rare to have been studied properly in dogs. Carprofen has been implicated in some cases of liver failure in dogs in the USA (5.2 cases / 10,000 doses).

Paracetamol produces specific hepatotoxic metabolites, particularly in cats. These toxic metabolites are usually mopped up by glutathione, but liver glutathione reserves can be quickly used up and liver damage results. Acetylcysteine can be given as a source of glutathione but prevention is better than cure - paracetamol is best avoided in both cats and dogs.

Agranulocytosis can occur, especially after phenylbutazone use in dogs, but is very rare.
